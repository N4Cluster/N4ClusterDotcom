# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

The primary visitor is an **independent restaurant owner/operator** running a single location, evaluating whether to leave a commission marketplace (DoorDash-style, 20–30% per order) or a flat-fee ordering SaaS competitor that charges $300–500/month regardless of volume. They think in kitchen ticket times, weekend-rush staffing, food-cost percentage, and take-home margin — not infrastructure or microservices. They land on this site stressed about what commissions or flat fees are actually costing them, and skeptical of vendor promises after being burned by long onboarding timelines or hidden fees before.

## Product Purpose

N4Cluster gives restaurants a branded direct-ordering channel, local discovery, AI-assisted growth, and delivery — priced as flat utility infrastructure ($99/month + $0.50/order) instead of a commission cut or a flat SaaS tax. It exists so an independent operator can keep the customer relationship, the data, and the margin a marketplace would otherwise take. Success is a visitor deciding to switch off their current marketplace or SaaS vendor: requesting a demo, or running the ROI calculator as the lower-commitment step toward that decision.

## Positioning

Flat utility pricing, not a commission or a subscription tax — the cost tracks usage instead of taking a percentage of revenue (like a marketplace) or charging a flat fee regardless of volume (like flat-fee ordering SaaS). Every page reinforces the same claim: this is infrastructure pricing, not extraction.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary and secondary CTA: Primary — Request a Demo / Get Started. Secondary — Calculate Your Savings (the ROI calculator), the lower-commitment fallback for a visitor not ready to talk to sales.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: Flat $99/month plus $0.50 per order. No commission. Ever.
- Belief ladder: (1) I'm losing real margin to a marketplace's commission or a flat-fee SaaS tax that doesn't scale with my volume. (2) N4Cluster's $99 + $0.50 pricing is simple and actually flat — not a teaser rate. (3) Switching won't cost me weeks of setup or a painful migration. (4) Requesting a demo doesn't lock me into anything.
- Proof on hand: none yet — the site is pre-launch/early-stage. The existing `/case-studies` page is explicitly framed as hypothetical design-partner pilots, not live customer results. Design should not imply social proof that doesn't exist yet (no fabricated logo strips, no testimonial quotes attributed to real-sounding but unverified customers).

## Brand Personality

Direct, no-nonsense, trustworthy — plainspoken operator-to-operator credibility. The site should read like a peer who has run a kitchen talking straight to another operator, not a SaaS vendor pitching a deck. This matches the authoritative, jargon-free copy voice already shipped across the site's pages.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS template**: purple-to-blue gradients, hero-metric cards, identical icon-tile feature grids, a tiny uppercase tracked eyebrow above every section. The default AI/SaaS look this site should never be mistaken for.
- **Consumer delivery-app aesthetic**: should not look or feel like DoorDash, Uber Eats, or a flashy consumer ordering app. This is a B2B infrastructure pitch to operators, not a consumer product.
- **Corporate enterprise/consulting site**: no stiff navy-suit stock photography, no dense feature-matrix tables, no jargon-heavy enterprise-software visual tropes.

## Design Principles

Every page reinforces one claim: flat utility pricing beats both commission extraction and a flat subscription tax, so no section should drift into a generic feature pitch that loses that thread. Speak like an operator peer, not a SaaS vendor — concrete numbers and kitchen/margin imagery earn their place over abstractions or jargon. Prove the price rather than just stating it: wherever the pitch is made, make the math (marketplace commission vs. flat-fee SaaS vs. N4Cluster) visible and concrete, not a bare claim. Never fabricate proof — no fake logos, no testimonial-shaped copy implying a real customer, until real proof exists. Make switching feel safe and fast at every step, since a visitor's biggest objection is setup-time fear, not price.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA baseline: minimum 4.5:1 contrast for body text (4.5:1 for placeholder text too, not the muted-gray default), 3:1 for large text, full keyboard navigation, and reduced-motion alternatives for every animation. No additional or stricter requirements specified.
